Elizabeth · Exploratory analysis

Demand follows a clear weekly rhythm.

Average sales by day of week

Key insight: Wednesday and Thursday consistently peak. Weekends dip by ~25%. Any model that ignores this weekly cycle will make systematic errors every week.

Monthly demand trend

Seasonal pattern: Summer (Jul–Aug) peaks, winter dips. This annual cycle is important context — models trained in winter will initially underestimate summer demand.

Ashkan · Demand forecasting

We tested three models. Here is how they work.

Model 1

Moving Average (MA-7)

What it does: Predicts tomorrow = average of the past 7 days. Dead simple.

Strength: Fast, no overfitting, naturally captures weekly cycles.

Weakness: Ignores trends. If demand is rising it always lags.

Model 2

Holt-Winters

What it does: Decomposes the series into level, trend, and weekly seasonality. Updates all three at every observation.

Strength: Handles trending + seasonal data well in theory.

Weakness: On high-variance products it overfits and adapts slowly.

Model 3

SARIMA(1,1,1)(1,1,1,7)

What it does: Full statistical time series model — captures trend, seasonality, and autocorrelation (how today's sales depend on yesterday's).

Strength: Most flexible and powerful of the three.

Weakness: Slower to fit; can overfit on noisy data.

How we evaluated them — 80/20 time-based split

Train on the first 80% of dates (~583 days). Test on the remaining 20% (~146 days). Models never see future data. Accuracy = MAE (Mean Absolute Error) in units — lower is better.

Why MA-7 wins: Fresh produce is bought out of habit. Demand is driven by stable weekly cycles — not complex long-term trends. A 7-day average captures that perfectly without overfitting.

Dmytro · Price analysis

Higher price = fewer sales.
But the sensitivity varies a lot.

Portfolio-level elasticity (β)

-0.40 overall β

1% price increase → 0.40% demand drop. Statistically significant (p < 0.0001).

Price-inelastic overall: The store has pricing power across the portfolio. Customers keep buying even if prices nudge up slightly.

7 elastic products (β < -1): Org. Cucumber, Loose Red Pepper, Aubergine, Broccoli, Snack Cucumbers, Cherry Tomatoes, Cucumber. Raising their prices risks a large volume drop.

3 inelastic products (β > -0.65): Carrots (-0.65), Iceberg Lettuce (-0.61), Romaine Hearts (-0.38). Safe to test a small price increase — customers won't notice.

Dmytro · Promotions

A data quality finding: zero promotions recorded.

⚠️
DISCOUNT = 0

For every product, across all 700+ days. The promotion flag was never activated.

Not a modelling failure. We caught it, investigated it, and are reporting it. Either no promotions ran in this period, or the POS system is not capturing the discount flag.

Action item: Ask the store's data team whether the DISCOUNT column is being populated. If promotions are run but not recorded, the data pipeline has a gap that affects all future analysis.

What elasticity tells us instead

Since we can't measure promotional lift directly, price elasticity serves as a proxy — it tells us which products would respond most strongly to a discount.

Best candidates for a promotion

High-elasticity products: Organic Cucumber (-2.44), Loose Red Pepper (-2.20), Aubergine (-2.01). A 10% price cut could boost volume by 20%+ based on the elasticity coefficient.

Avoid discounting these

Low-elasticity products: Carrots, Iceberg Lettuce, Romaine Hearts. Discounting them generates minimal extra volume — you just give away margin.

Causal caveat

Elasticity estimates are correlational. Seasonality, weekday effects, and competitor pricing are not fully controlled. A proper A/B pricing test would give cleaner causal estimates.

Nadiia · Inventory trade-off

90% vs 95%: is the extra stock worth it?

Reorder points — top 10 products

The trade-off

Going from 90% to 95% costs on average 81 extra units per product. For perishables, those may spoil. The decision: is the cost of a stockout greater than the cost of holding and wasting 81 units?

Aubergine outlier: 95% safety stock = 399 units vs 149 at 90%. Very high demand variance makes the 95% level extremely costly for this specific product.

Shelf life caveat: The shelf life columns in the dataset were empty. Reorder points should be capped at daily demand × shelf life days. For our high-volume products, the reorder points represent 2–5 days of stock — within typical fresh produce windows — but this should be verified.

Recommendation: Implement the 90% reorder points now. Review quarterly as seasonal demand patterns shift.
